☄️ Magnetic Confinement of CMEs

← Back to Projects

Motivation and Context

AB Dor (HD 36705) is a young solar-mass star with an age of ~120 Myr and surface magnetic fields of kG strength— approximately 100–1000 times stronger than the Sun. Despite theoretical predictions that strong overlying magnetic fields prevent CME eruptions, Veronig et al. (2021) recently detected coronal dimming signatures in EUV/X-ray, suggesting eruptive CMEs at high latitudes on AB Dor. A particularly notable 1994 EUVE observation showed dimming lasting 13.3 hours, a duration comparable to AB Dor's rotation period of 0.51 days, implying the CME originated from a high-latitude region that remained in view as the star rotated.

To investigate the eruption and confinement of CMEs in this magnetically extreme environment, we conducted a parametric magnetohydrodynamic (MHD) study, varying flux rope properties and injection location to determine the conditions necessary for eruption.

Methodology

Stellar Wind Model

We employed the Alfvén Wave Solar Model (AWSOM) within the 3D MHD code BATS-R-US to simulate the steady-state stellar wind of AB Dor. The model solves the MHD equations assuming Alfvén wave heating. Input parameters include the surface magnetic field derived from Zeeman-Doppler Imaging (ZDI) observations from December 2002 (Hussain et al., 2007), AB Dor's mass (0.86 M☉), radius (0.96 R☉), and rotation period (0.514 d).

CME Flux Rope Model

CMEs were introduced using the parametric flux rope model of Titov & Démoulin (1999) with a toroidal geometry. The flux rope was initialized at the inner boundary with footpoints anchored to the stellar surface. We varied three parameters across 21 simulations:

Table 2: Flux Rope Parameter Space
Parameter Range
Flux rope mass 3.5×10¹⁴ to 3.5×10¹⁸ g
Central magnetic field strength 100 to 1000 G
Location Open or closed field regions at ~60° latitude
Constant Geometric Parameters: Major radius 128 Mm, minor radius 20 Mm, depth 1 Mm, consistent with previous stellar CME simulations.

Results

Eruption Statistics

Of the 21 simulated CMEs, 11 resulted in eruptive events while 10 remained confined. CMEs initiated in open-field regions showed a higher propensity to erupt. We identified critical thresholds for eruption:

Table 3: Eruption Thresholds by Magnetic Geometry
Injection Region Eruptive / Confined Minimum Field Strength
Open-field Higher propensity ~200 G
Closed-field Lower propensity ~800 G
Critical Discovery: The 4× threshold difference (200 G vs. 800 G) between open and closed field regions reflects additional magnetic tension that must be overcome in closed geometries. Field geometry is the dominant control on eruption probability.

Energetics and Dynamics

After one hour of evolution, eruptive CME velocities ranged from 220 to 3700 km/s. The most energetic model (maximum flux rope mass and field strength in an open region) reached velocities of ~3800 km/s with a total CME mass of 4.1×10¹⁸ g and kinetic energy of 2.9×10³⁵ erg.

Confined CMEs exhibited significantly lower velocities and kinetic energies. CMEs in closed-field regions showed systematically lower velocities than those in open regions for identical flux rope parameters, reflecting increased magnetic tension from the overlying field.

Key Finding: Eruptive open-field CMEs reach velocities 5–10× higher than their closed-field counterparts for equivalent flux rope parameters. Energy differences exceed six orders of magnitude.

Magnetic Field Dependence

CME energetics correlated strongly with poloidal magnetic flux. For a given flux rope mass, increasing the poloidal flux led to higher CME masses, velocities, and kinetic energies. Power-law fits were derived for open and closed field regions separately, revealing different scaling behavior. The field geometry proved critical: identical flux rope parameters injected in open versus closed field regions produced energetic differences up to six orders of magnitude.

Interpretation

Our simulations demonstrate that CMEs can successfully erupt on AB Dor despite its strong surface magnetic field, but with critical dependence on field geometry. The threshold difference between open and closed regions (200 G vs. 800 G) reflects the additional magnetic tension that must be overcome in closed regions. CMEs in closed-field regions that do achieve eruption exhibit significantly lower velocities and kinetic energies, with a substantial fraction of energy expended to break open the overlying field.

The observation that the most energetic eruptions occurred in open-field regions suggests that high-latitude polar active regions on AB Dor, combined with the local open-field geometry, are favorable sites for CME eruptions. This is consistent with the 1994 EUVE dimming detection and the known presence of polar spots on AB Dor.

Physical Insight: High-latitude polar active regions in open-field geometry provide the optimal conditions for energetic CME eruptions on AB Dor. This explains the 1994 EUVE observation as a naturally expected phenomenon in magnetically extreme young stars.
