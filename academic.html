High-latitude coronal mass ejections on the young solar-like star AB Dor

K. M. Strickert, D. Evensberget, A. A. Vidotto

Monthly Notices of the Royal Astronomical Society 2024 Vol. 533, pp. 1156–1165

This work investigates coronal mass ejections (CMEs) on AB Dor, a young solar-like star with a surface magnetic field 100-1000 times stronger than the Sun's. Using 3D magnetohydrodynamic simulations, I modeled 21 CME scenarios at high latitudes to understand how strong magnetic fields constrain eruptions.

Technical approach:

  • Implemented Alfvén Wave Solar Model (AWSOM) in 3D MHD code BATS-R-US to model AB Dor's stellar wind
  • Used spectropolarimetric observations and Zeeman-Doppler imaging to derive surface magnetic field map
  • Injected magnetic flux ropes at high latitudes (~60°) in both open and closed magnetic field regions
  • Parametrically varied flux rope properties: mass (3.5×10¹⁴ - 3.5×10¹⁸ g) and magnetic field strength (100-1000 G)
  • Simulated 21 different CME configurations with varying combinations of location, mass, and field strength
  • Analyzed CME morphology, eruption vs. confinement, velocity, mass, and kinetic energies at 1 hour post-injection

Key findings:

  • CMEs in open magnetic field regions are significantly more likely to erupt
  • CMEs in closed magnetic field regions require high free magnetic energies (≥ 3×10³⁵ erg) to escape
  • Magnetic tension limits kinetic energy in confined regions, causing slower propagation
  • High-latitude eruptive CMEs likely occur in regions of open magnetic field, consistent with AB Dor's observed polar active regions
  • Maximum eruptive CME speeds reached ~3700 km/s, five times higher than background wind velocity

Galactic Dynamics and the spread of Galactic Civilisations

Markus Strickert

Lund Observatory, Lund University June 2020 15 higher education credits

This bachelor's thesis addresses a fascinating question: can a single interstellar civilisation colonise the galaxy faster than new civilisations can develop? The work simulates how technologically advanced civilisations might spread through a region of the galaxy by colonising nearby planets during close encounters with other star systems.

Technical approach:

  • Modeled epicyclic motion of 200-1000 stars in the solar neighbourhood using galactic dynamics
  • Tracked close encounters between stars using stochastic simulations
  • Simulated planetary evolution with probability distributions governing abiogenesis and civilisation development
  • Varied steepness parameter N to control the probability distribution for life development
  • Compared scenarios with single spreading civilisations versus multiple stationary civilisations

Key findings:

  • First technologically developed civilisation emerged between 270 million and 4.8 billion years, depending on the steepness parameter N
  • Colonization by spreading civilisations followed exponential growth initially, then leveled off as available planets saturated
  • With 1000 stars and N=3, a single spreading civilisation colonised 89% of all planets within 12 gigayears
  • With lower steepness parameters (N=0), stationary civilisations dominated, colonising 99-99.7% of planets
  • Star density significantly affects the competition: higher density favours spreading civilisations
  • The model suggests that rapid galactic colonisation is possible only under specific conditions combining high star density and favourable evolution parameters
